Rogers Park Landmarks

The Mile of Murals

Here we are at the northwest most part of the "Mile of Murals", we can either go left or right of this intersection to walk south down to see the rest of the murals along Glenwood Ave! Launched in 2007, Rogers Park's Miles of Murals is an ongoing series promoting and celebrating the artistic identity that is central to the neighborhood's rich culture. Discover the history of Rogers Park, which predates the founding of the City of Chicago, and learn about how it grew to become one of the city's most diverse communities today. This tour honors the artists who have contributed to the Mile of Murals project and discusses the social and community concerns explored in the 14 works spanning concrete embankments, viaducts, and overpasses along Glenwood Avenue

The New 400 Theater

Below we have another vital element of Rogers Park's history, The New 400 Theater. The theater was originally built in 1912 and currently stands as the oldest operating theater of Chicago. Beginning in 1912, this small theater has seen much of our American history. From participating in the Four-Minute Men Program during World War I--which was a program that honored people of the war effort--to striving to uplift spirits of the Rogers Park locals during the Great Depression. Despite surviving over 100 years of history, The 400 Theater is looking at a significant decline post-pandemic. In spite of their situation, the theater never refrains from raising their very cheap prices. Even still can students and locals alike come to the theater and enjoy watching a new movie from prices ranging from $6-$9. At the moment, the theater is still alive and running, but they look to the people of Rogers Park to help them continue the course of their story.

Ralph Arnold Gallery

Keepscapes features the work of Loyola seniors studying art and visual communications. The work shown represents the culmination of each student's experiences as a Fine Arts major. With a variety of approaches to making across multiple disciplines, this exhibition represents the ambition, passion, and activism that make Loyolans stand out. These students are challenging assumptions, mining the culture around them, and creating meaning through innovative design and compelling investigations of space, society and the self.
